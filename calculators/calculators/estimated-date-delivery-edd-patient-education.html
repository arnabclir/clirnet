Important to Know

Your due date is an estimate, not a deadline. It's normal for babies to arrive 2 weeks before or after this date. Most babies are born between 37 and 42 weeks of pregnancy.

Your healthcare provider will confirm this date with an ultrasound scan, usually during your first prenatal visit or at your dating ultrasound.

What to Expect at Different Stages
  • Weeks 1-12 (First Trimester): First appointment, dating ultrasound, screening tests
  • Weeks 13-27 (Second Trimester): Anatomy ultrasound, continued prenatal visits
  • Weeks 28-40 (Third Trimester): More frequent visits, labor preparation, positioning
  • 37+ weeks: Baby is full-term and ready to be born at any time
  • 42 weeks: Your doctor may recommend induction if labor hasn't started
Next Steps
  • Schedule your first prenatal appointment
  • Confirm this date with your healthcare provider
  • Start prenatal vitamins (if not already)
  • Prepare for your dating ultrasound
  • Share your due date with family and friends

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I don't know my last period date?

Your doctor can estimate your due date using an ultrasound. This is especially accurate if done early in pregnancy. Many women get a dating ultrasound between 8-14 weeks to confirm the due date.

Will my baby be born exactly on the due date?

No. Due dates are estimates. Only about 5% of babies are born exactly on their due date. Most babies are born within 2 weeks (before or after) the estimated due date. Babies born anytime from 37-42 weeks are considered full-term.

What if my cycle is longer or shorter than 28 days?

If your cycles are different, your actual due date may vary slightly. Your ultrasound is the best way to confirm the due date, especially early in pregnancy.

When should I expect labor?

Early labor (37-38 weeks) is possible but babies born before 37 weeks may need special care. Full-term is 39-40 weeks. Most healthy pregnancies continue to 40 weeks or slightly beyond.

Should I be concerned if my baby isn't born by the due date?

Not necessarily. Your doctor will monitor you and your baby. If you go past 42 weeks, your doctor may recommend induction, but many pregnancies naturally go a few days past the due date.
